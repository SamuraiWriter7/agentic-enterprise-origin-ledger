# Cross-Department Execution Trace

Version: 0.3.0

## 1. Purpose

The Cross-Department Execution Trace records how an approved AI-agent skill
moves through an enterprise process involving multiple departments.

The trace preserves:

- the originating enterprise knowledge record;
- the approved skill binding;
- every department participating in the execution;
- the sequence of agent, system, and human actions;
- authorized data access;
- decisions and their supporting evidence;
- human review, correction, approval, or override;
- policy checks;
- restrictions carried through handoffs;
- operational outcomes and business effects.

The record exists to prevent a cross-department workflow from becoming a set
of disconnected logs that cannot explain why an outcome occurred.

## 2. Core principle

> Every consequential enterprise outcome must remain connected to its origin,
> execution path, human authority, and evidence.

A trace is not complete merely because system events were logged. It must
preserve causal continuity across organizational boundaries.

## 3. Trace model

```text
Enterprise Knowledge Origin
          ↓
Knowledge-to-Agent Skill Binding
          ↓
Execution trigger
          ↓
Department A: data and recommendation
          ↓
Handoff with inherited restrictions
          ↓
Department B: human review and decision
          ↓
Handoff with inherited restrictions
          ↓
Department C: analysis or operational action
          ↓
Outcome, audit, and business effects
```

## 4. Normative requirements

A conforming Cross-Department Execution Trace MUST:

1. reference the originating knowledge record;
2. reference the approved skill binding and version;
3. verify the integrity of the referenced binding;
4. identify the initiating department and actor;
5. identify every participating department and its authority scope;
6. record every interdepartmental handoff;
7. state which restrictions were carried through each handoff;
8. record execution steps in causal order;
9. distinguish agent, human, team, and system actors;
10. record data access purpose, classification, authorization, and policy;
11. record consequential decisions and their evidence;
12. expose the human-authority status of each decision;
13. record human corrections, approvals, rejections, overrides, or escalations;
14. record policy evaluations and whether they were blocking;
15. record the operational outcome and unresolved issues;
16. preserve origin attribution and permission propagation;
17. retain an integrity record and lifecycle status.

A conforming implementation MUST NOT:

1. treat departmental receipt as permission to discard upstream restrictions;
2. hide a human modification to an agent recommendation;
3. combine agent recommendations and human approvals into one indistinguishable event;
4. record a consequential action without an evidence path;
5. mark causality complete when intermediate handoffs or decisions are missing;
6. use business-effect estimates as verified value;
7. delete a trace merely because the operational case has closed.

## 5. Department route

The department route defines the organizational path of execution.

Each participant declares:

- department identity;
- role in the execution;
- authority scope.

Each handoff declares:

- sender and receiver;
- transfer time;
- payload summary;
- routing reason;
- acceptance status;
- accepting actor;
- restrictions carried forward.

A handoff is therefore not just a message transfer. It is a transfer of
context, responsibility, and constraints.

## 6. Execution steps

Execution steps are ordered using the `sequence` field.

Each step records:

- the responsible department;
- timing;
- performing actor;
- step type;
- whether the bound skill was invoked;
- input and output references;
- status;
- parent step where applicable.

The trace should preserve the difference between:

- data access;
- analysis;
- recommendation;
- human review;
- approval;
- operational action;
- handoff;
- monitoring.

## 7. Human authority

A human intervention is recorded separately from an agent decision.

This separation is required to show:

- whether the human accepted the recommendation;
- whether the human changed it;
- what existed before the intervention;
- what existed afterward;
- who possessed authority for the final action.

The protocol does not assume that every human intervention improves an agent
output. It records the intervention so that the result can later be audited.

## 8. Policy evaluation

Policy evaluations may be marked as blocking.

A blocking policy check means the execution must not proceed when the result is
`fail`.

Typical policy checks include:

- agent advisory boundaries;
- origin and attribution preservation;
- data-access scope;
- human-approval requirements;
- confidentiality restrictions;
- validated equipment or customer scope.

## 9. Business effects

The outcome may record business effects such as:

- time saved;
- cost avoided;
- risk reduced;
- quality improved;
- downtime avoided;
- revenue supported;
- compliance supported.

Each effect must state whether it is:

- estimated;
- observed;
- verified;
- not measured.

Version 0.3 records effects but does not allocate their value.

## 10. Audit completeness

The audit summary distinguishes:

- `trace_complete`: required events and records are present;
- `causality_complete`: the recorded events form a defensible cause-and-effect path;
- `attribution_preserved`: origin attribution remained attached;
- `permissions_propagated`: upstream restrictions survived each handoff.

A trace may be structurally complete while causal interpretation remains
incomplete. Implementations should not collapse these concepts.

## 11. Relationship to other versions

```text
v0.1 Enterprise Knowledge Origin Record
  ↓
v0.2 Knowledge-to-Agent Skill Binding
  ↓
v0.3 Cross-Department Execution Trace
  ↓
v0.4 Human Contribution Attribution
  ↓
v0.5 Enterprise Value Return Ledger
```

Version 0.3 forms the Trace and Audit layer.

It supplies future versions with evidence about:

- which human knowledge was used;
- which skill was invoked;
- which departments contributed;
- where humans corrected or approved agent outputs;
- what business effects followed.

## 12. Non-goals

Version 0.3 does not:

- calculate employee performance;
- determine legal liability;
- assign ownership;
- calculate royalty percentages;
- prove that an agent caused every observed business effect;
- replace security logging;
- replace enterprise records-management requirements;
- authorize autonomous execution.

Version 0.3 answers the question:

> What happened across the enterprise, why did it happen, whose authority
> changed the outcome, and did the original restrictions survive?
